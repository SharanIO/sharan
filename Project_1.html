Design and Development of a Robotic System for Soft Tissue Manipulation

Robotics Mechanical Design Computer Vision Biomechanics

In the realm of medical robotics, handling delicate tissues with precision presents unique engineering challenges. I developed a robotic system that combines innovative mechanical design with adaptive control to achieve gentle and effective tissue manipulation. Through systematic iteration and testing, I created an end-effector featuring a novel passive compliance mechanism that naturally adapts to varying tissue geometries. The system integrates pneumatic actuation with mechanical elements, enabling consistent and safe interaction across different tissue surfaces while maintaining procedural effectiveness.

30N Force Control Implemented precise force limitation system
4 Design Iterations Each solving specific technical challenges
100% Coverage Achieved full surface manipulation

Key Challenges & Design Requirements

Tissue Handling

  • Apply precise, repeatable force without tissue damage
  • Maximum safe force limit: 30 Newtons
  • Adapt to changes in tissue shape and properties

Design Requirements

  • Cost-effective production
  • Easy cleaning and sterilization
  • Complete surface coverage
  • Adaptability to various lung sizes/shapes
  • Modular design for maintenance

From Concept to Reality

Translating the delicate task of tissue manipulation into a reliable robotic system required a methodical approach to solving multiple engineering challenges. Through four major design iterations, each focused on specific aspects of the problem, I developed a system that could effectively balance precision, safety, and reliability.

Each iteration brought new insights about the complex interplay between mechanical design, force control, and tissue interaction. What began as a basic pneumatic system evolved into a sophisticated end-effector capable of adapting to varying tissue properties.

Initial Prototype

The project began with exploring various approaches to gentle tissue manipulation. Drawing inspiration from manual massage techniques, I investigated different gripper designs including soft robotic systems and therapeutic devices. This exploration led to selecting a kneading mechanism that mimicked manual manipulation techniques.

Key Innovations

  • Pneumatic actuator system for controlled kneading motion
  • Spring-loaded joints enabling surface adaptation

Critical Insights

Initial testing revealed the need for broader surface coverage and more consistent contact distribution, leading to a focus on multi-point contact mechanisms in subsequent designs.

Multi-Point Contact System

Building on initial insights, this iteration introduced a six-finger design arranged in a circular pattern. Each finger incorporated passive joints with torsional springs, enabling better adaptation to tissue surfaces while maintaining consistent contact pressure.

Key Innovations

  • Radial arrangement of six passive fingers maximizing coverage area
  • Integrated torsional springs for controlled compliance
  • Enhanced surface adaptation through passive mechanisms

Critical Insights

Testing demonstrated improved surface coverage but highlighted the need for smoother contact transitions and more uniform pressure distribution during manipulation.

Modular Roller System

This iteration focused on improving contact quality through integrated roller mechanisms. The design featured removable fingers with ball-socket joints, allowing for easy customization and maintenance. Steel rollers were incorporated to ensure smooth tissue interaction.

Key Innovations

  • Modular finger design with quick-release mechanisms
  • Integrated steel rollers with 3D-printed ball-socket joints
  • Enhanced adaptability through customizable contact points

Critical Insights

While the roller system improved contact quality, testing revealed the need for broader coverage and more uniform pressure distribution across larger tissue surfaces.

Tree-Structure Final Design

The final iteration introduced a tree-structured finger arrangement with multiple contact points per branch. This design maximizes surface coverage while maintaining adaptability. A custom fixture system was developed to stabilize tissue during manipulation.

Key Innovations

  • Tree-structured finger arrangement with integrated rollers
  • 3D-printed test fixture for holding the tissue
  • Custom-designed tissue fixturing system

Critical Insights

Final testing validated the design's effectiveness in achieving uniform contact pressure and consistent manipulation across tissue surfaces. The modular structure proved highly adaptable to various tissue geometries.

Testing & Validation

Each iteration of the design underwent rigorous testing to validate its performance and identify areas for improvement. These tests provided crucial insights that guided the evolution of the system.

Initial Prototype Test

Demonstration of initial kneading mechanism comparing manual massage techniques with robotic implementation.


Key metrics:

  • Contact pressure adaptation: ±15% variation
  • Surface coverage: 45% of target area
  • Kneading frequency: 0.5-2 Hz adjustable range

Tree Structure Performance

Testing of the branched finger design with integrated rollers. Results showed:


  • Coverage increased to 85% of target surface
  • Uniform contact maintained across 92% of engagement area
  • Successful adaptation to ±20° surface variations

Complete System Validation

Final prototype testing with multi-roller contact system and modular fingers. Performance highlights:


  • Full surface coverage achieved with modular finger design
  • Contact pressure consistency within ±10%
  • Successful tissue manipulation across varied geometries

Robotic System Integration

Integration testing with Powerball Schunk robotic arm demonstrating:


  • 6-DOF manipulation capabilities
  • Automated trajectory execution
  • Synchronized actuation of dual pneumatic systems
  • Real-time position control accuracy: ±0.5mm

Advanced Development

Building upon our initial success with the passive end-effector, we explored advanced techniques for improving tissue manipulation through 3D modeling and robotic integration.

3D Tissue Modeling

Using an Intel RealSense camera, we captured detailed point cloud data of lung tissue to understand its structural properties and design optimal fixturing solutions.

  • Data Collection
  • Point Cloud Processing
  • Surface Reconstruction
  • Fixture Design Optimization

Robotic Integration

The passive end-effector was integrated with a Powerball Schunk 6-DOF robot to explore advanced manipulation capabilities.

  • Robot: Powerball Schunk 6-DOF
  • End-Effector: Custom Passive Design
  • Control System: ROS Integration
  • Workspace: Adaptable to Various Tissue Sizes
