Design and Development of a Robotic System for Soft Tissue Manipulation

Robotics Mechanical Design Computer Vision Biomechanics

In the world of robotics, precisely controlling force is one of our greatest challenges. While robots excel at moving with sub-millimeter accuracy, asking them to apply just the right amount of pressure – like a skilled therapist performing a massage – presents a fascinating engineering puzzle. I tackled this challenge by developing a robotic system that combines the precision of automation with the gentle touch needed for soft tissue manipulation. Through innovative mechanical design and systematic iteration, I created an end-effector that can apply controlled pressure across tissue surfaces while ensuring safety and effectiveness. The system combines pneumatic actuation with passive elements, allowing it to adapt to varying tissue properties while maintaining consistent force control.

Key Challenges & Design Requirements

Tissue Handling

  • Apply precise, repeatable force without tissue damage
  • Maximum safe force limit: 30 Newtons
  • Adapt to changes in tissue shape and properties

Design Requirements

  • Cost-effective production
  • Easy cleaning and sterilization
  • Complete surface coverage
  • Adaptability to various lung sizes/shapes
  • Modular design for maintenance

From Concept to Reality

Translating the delicate task of tissue manipulation into a reliable robotic system required a methodical approach to solving multiple engineering challenges. Through four major design iterations, each focused on specific aspects of the problem, I developed a system that could effectively balance precision, safety, and reliability.

Each iteration brought new insights about the complex interplay between mechanical design, force control, and tissue interaction. What began as a basic pneumatic system evolved into a sophisticated end-effector capable of adapting to varying tissue properties while maintaining precise force control.

Initial Prototype

The first challenge was establishing basic force control while ensuring the system could interact safely with tissue surfaces. I approached this by designing a pneumatic actuation system combined with passive joints for initial force absorption.

Key Innovations

  • Single-rod pneumatic actuator for controlled force application
  • Basic passive joints for initial surface adaptation
  • Preliminary force control implementation

Critical Insights

Testing revealed the need for better surface adaptation and more uniform force distribution across the tissue surface, guiding the next iteration's focus on enhanced contact mechanisms.

Testing & Validation

Each iteration of the design underwent rigorous testing to validate its performance and identify areas for improvement. These tests provided crucial insights that guided the evolution of the system.

Force Application Test

Advanced Development

Building upon our initial success with the passive end-effector, we explored advanced techniques for improving tissue manipulation through 3D modeling and robotic integration.

3D Tissue Modeling

Using an Intel RealSense camera, we captured detailed point cloud data of lung tissue to understand its structural properties and design optimal fixturing solutions.

  • Data Collection
  • Point Cloud Processing
  • Surface Reconstruction
  • Fixture Design Optimization

Robotic Integration

The passive end-effector was integrated with a Powerball Schunk 6-DOF robot to explore advanced manipulation capabilities.

System Specifications

  • Robot: Powerball Schunk 6-DOF
  • End-Effector: Custom Passive Design
  • Control System: ROS Integration
  • Workspace: Adaptable to Various Tissue Sizes
